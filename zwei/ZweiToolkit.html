Notes on Ordering of Gradient Elements

* For the storytelling, I'm adopting what I dubbed the "Japanese pre-storytelling" priorities that do not address the storywriting and visuals until after establishing characters (and their sonzai-kan), world (or sekai-kan), and dramatic premise (or settei, lit. not just "setting" but "establishment", "creation").

* Likewise, we have Death to the 3-Act Structure talk:

1. Develop characters per Holly Lisle first, as characters are remembered far more greatly (for their characterization, not plot roles!) than narrative. ("We interact personally with characters, but we only watch plots impersonally.")

- This also resonates with the Japanese pre-storytelling priorities of cast-before-plot.

2. Align narrative with gameplay structure: UX, loops, levels/missions.

3. Build your story's dramatic rhythms around those needs, not 3 acts.

 Geographer's Worldbuilding

Optional/Wishlist/Misc.

Use the 5+3 below to flesh out the Kasavin choice of theme and tone = atmosphere!

- PRINCIPLES: A 5-Part Recipe for Worldbuilding

A. A suggestion of familiarity: even Middle Earth has normal mountains.

B. A hint of complex systems: artifacts in env, hints in narrative, characters showing up revealing aspects of world.

C. A dash of cultural evidence: aliens, animals, whatever it is -- the Forerunner idea that a group inhabited an area.

//HIGHEST RISK ACTIVITY HERE! Because you let the design and art teams loose on a vision to "backfill" it, i.e. clutter.

- But #1, don't be lazy -- add things with a purpose. e.g. adding Buddhist prayer wheels to a game without Buddhism!

- And #2, ask questions and question assumptions -- why does it look that way?

> BASICALLY: remove the randomness that easily creeps into cluttering areas.

D. A big chunk of logical consistency: Kasavin! Breaking this will shatter the magic circle in a heartbeat.

E. A pinch of topology: not unlike network topologies, i.e. connectedness of attributes, places, qualities, traits.

//Why Tolkien went through finding out why humans, elves, dwarves all coexist in his world when he wrote Silmallirion.

> Can imply a bigger world than simply what's present!

- APPLICATION: 3 Steps to Apply Recipe Principles

1. Determine Context: e.g. Real World vs Fictional World

- RW: familiarity there, but comes with expectations. (Extreme: Days of Fallujah)

- World Creation: REPLICATION and AUTHENTICITY.

- World Consumption: REALISM and experience that is GENUINE.

- Issues: augmentation (e.g. enhancing a sacred site), interpretation (e.g. Age of Empires and Korean patch), accuracy (e.g. "that's Clinton"), appropriateness (e.g. gunning Sikhs at their sacred site in Hitman).

- FW: freedom without expectation constraint, but no familiarity.

- World Creation: IMAGINATION and DISTINCTION.

- World Consumption: DISCOVERY and experience of ESCAPISM.

- Issues: generalization (e.g. Smite treating Kali like old Roman and Greek gods), emulation ("desert cultures" looking awfully Arabic-Bedouin, modeling an evil boss to look like a real-world deity that's benevolent), allegory (e.g. Dervish -> Arbiter being a title in Sufi Islam like Pope being so specific in Christianity, and pretty-American Master Chief going to destroy the arbiter's faction--with 9/11 just having happened), artifacts (e.g. a moai being so specific to eastern island culture where their character design angered Polynesian cultures).

2. Determine Complexity: How much is needed to actually make the world serve the narrative and experience goals?

- Geographers learn how to do this from deconstructing our own world!

- //May need all, some, OR none of these levels of abstraction:

a. Climatology & Atmosphere

b. Geophysical: geomorphology (how landmasses form), hydrology, tectonics (Tolkien's world lacks here), gravity, etc.

c. Biosphere

d. Demographics: species, genders, ages, ethnicities, etc.

e. Cultural Identities: language, history/lore, symbology, etc.

f. Cultural Systems: faiths, politics, economies, transportation.

- Biggest problem layers: history, faith, cultural identities and systems, geopolitics.

- History: For RW this is persistent but volatile (so choose wisely), for FW beware echoing RW events.

- Faith: with RW religions be careful about what you choose to portray and why, in FW avoid drawing on RW religions (esp. Roman Catholicism being used in bad guy alien priest designs).

- Identities: representing a RW in off-the-shelf stereotypes (going back to lazy creation), in FW to make a group identity feel more real draw on languages, history/lore, symbology, etc.

- Systems: tensions between nationalities and cultures on different issues in RW, emulation for FW like culturally-specific gestures (bullhorns mean "I'm sleeping with your wife" in Italy).

- Geopolitics: for RW governments strongly watch this (China), for FW it's augmenting, enhancing, and borrowing geopolitical scenarios. (Several nations ban games for not showing locales as part of a nation, e.g. Tibet and China.)

3. Create with Intent: Build the World's Layers

- There's political correctness, but there's also the tension against needing to make profit in areas that will not accept your game without world changes.

 10 Ways of Seeing

Optional/Wishlist/Misc.

Ways of Seeing

1. Identify what gameplay occurs in the space.

- What are the game mechanics supported?

2. Look UP as you walk around, notice visual elements, esp. art that contrasts the rest of the env. or somehow calls attention to itself.

- Also look DOWN: is verticality used in reverse to make you feel in danger?

3. Sense the space by its size, shape, and how it interacts with light, sound, and other spaces.

- How do the lighting/sound conditions make you FEEL?

4. Analyze level pacing.

- Does it usher you through quickly?

- Are there chances to explore?

- Are these required OR bonuses for extra curiosity?

5. Is there one gameplay style reflected in this level, OR are multiple supported?

- e.g. A deathmatch map with places for snipers, but also rushers, turtles, etc.

- Does the game play poorly for one style vs. another?

6. HOW does the space express game narrative?

- Is narrative a backdrop?

- Does exploring the level tell you about the gameworld?

- Are narrative events scripted to occur around you?

- Or are there cutscenes?

7. Examine any historical/gameplay PRECEDENTS.

- What kinds of spatial experiences were in those games?

8. Analyze composition, proportions, rhythms of env art elements.

9. HOW does level geometry compare with avatar movement abilities?

- Is everything well within their capabilities?

- OR does the level space challenge these measurements?

- Is there anything outside these capabilities?

- If so, does game offer any way to expand these abilities?

10. What env art elements REPEAT?

- Are they interactive?

- If so, do they correspond to a specific gameplay mechanic?

Threes GDC Talk on Tutorials: 4 Ingredients!

* Teach, Comfort, Excite, & Respect the Player

* Instructing the player: fails at last two.

* Stay out of their way: fails at first two (ex. popup).

* Present a safe space: all but the third (ex. Mario).

- Can excite! (Uncharted 2 train; Kratos v. hydra.)

* Make goals into puzzles: all four succeeded!

- i.e. Present learning a system as a puzzle.

- NOT following big blinking arrows.

- These make the player process 0% of it.

- e.g. Threes: "you can xxx", NOT "swipe left here".

- Declarative hint, not an imperative solution.

KEY: which one among the above recipe ingredients you lean on more depends on you game--playtest! You WILL have to remake this over and over, even if you hit upon all 4 lenses!

- Threes left a lot uncovered by the tutorial, because he wanted to raise a lot of questions you could explore and find answers to by playing.

//Five Mechanic Types: PEPSI

PHYSICAL 1. Physics: Governs Spatial or Temporal Behaviors (jumping, moving)

RESOURCES 2. Economy: Governs Resource Transactions (acquiring, consuming, trading)

TRAVERSAL 3. Progress: Governs Traversing Game's Spatial Graph (keys, switches, locks) <- LDs!

TRANSFORM 4. Station: Governs Consequences of Placements on Game's Spatial Graph (ex. units in an RTS)

SOCIAL 5. Interpersonal: Governs Interactions b/t Players (reinforcing gifting, referral, participation, alliance/betrayal) -- how to have a character emulate a player?

//Discrete (int#s) vs Continuous (FP#s) Mechanics

1. Physics: Spatial and Temporal -- timing, dexterity, precision tests

2. Economics: Transactions of Resource (acquire, consume, trade)

3. Progression: Blockers (lock) and Un[b]lockers (key, switch) -- tied to the LD but seems it also includes general progression thru graph theory structures a la Koster.

4. Positional: Pros/Cons to being placed in a given area (ex. units in an RTS).

5. Social: Afford/Modulate Player-Player Interaction

1. Gameplay progression != difficulty progression.

2. Gameplay progression == structuring (PACING!) the distribution of game mechanics, mission duration +deaths, gameplay and audiovisual rewards, and difficulty/risk so as to not overwhelm or underwhelm players with too much or too little to continue playing.

2a. Progression of Game Mechanics: keeps player from being initially overwhelmed, e.g. if they were given the full Zelda inventory at once.

- Style 1: Gated Access to mechanics until later in game.

- Style 2: Directed Gameplay affording everything but missions/levels only require a gradually broadening amount of them.

2b. Progression of Mission Duration: harder enemies, longer distances, or harsher penalties (e.g. deaths/checkpoints) that gradually elongate the experience.

2c. Progression of Ancillary Rewards: environmental progression (scripted action events, landmarks and vistas with scaling intensity), also trophies/medals.

2d. Progression of Practical Rewards: see if Gamification book speaks to these points,

- First, these are the most effective way to retain players.

- Second, the best rate is to seem initially fast with a few nice awards very soon after starting the game, and then grow slower with time.

- Third, players need to transparently understand the rewards system: when rewards come (automatically or not) and at what price if any.

- Fourth, players should glimpse future rewards to anticipate and keep [re]playing.

2e. Progression of Difficulty/Risk: needs a curve.

* The Real Problem: balancing the dynamics of progression as project scope rises. Ideally, the progression is designed and planned early enough to bring the producer and team leads on board into backing such an approach, but on first generation engines often certain elements remain unknown until deep into production (e.g. how different AI behaviors affect difficulty). So, it is best to both plan the progression in advance as much as possible (early production), and to set a planned checkpoint later on to update the progression structure with formerly unknown elements, and finally to set multiple review and feedback loops in the content to make sure the progression structure is supported, followed and satisfactory to focus testers.

> "Structure the gameplay progression early on and to build content that fits that structure from the start. Realistically, the goal for us game designers should not be to build a Nintendo-worthy gameplay experience (it has taken them 20 years to get there), but to build the most enjoyable experience for the specific game we are working on and ultimately to make our users excited enough to play (and buy) our game. Then all will be right in the [game] world and [hopefully] the peasants will rejoice. M’kay? "

* DIALOGUE: two characters never talk about things that both of them already bloody know. //So solve it by making the inquiry not just more realistic but necessary. But the opposite problem is too much expository dialogue -- too many question marks in the screenplay. Dialogue isn't just for moving plot along though: moreover, like action, it's an opportunity to [indirectly] reveal character [as a byproduct of it]. It's NOT conversation, it's a dialogue -- sharing opinions on a topic. When dialogue neither advances plot or reveals character, you get The Room. Dialogue is only, then, as good as its character. Get into their motivations and headspace, and ask what they would say to show those to us. (SOUNDS A LOT LIKE GAMEPLAY!)  Concept Art x Dumpsters

Notably echoed by Insomniac's lead environment artist at gamasutra.com/view/news/300314/

Dying Light

* Promo v. Concept Art: former meant to be eye candy and draw attention (made at end, the cherry on top), latter meant to define the vision (at start, the cake). This talk at Digital Dragons was about FUNCTIONAL concept art, the kind you don't see as often in image search results or ever on ArtStation.

* Dumpsters and lack of detail in th ebrief that can't include all the uses of a AAA environment art asset. => The humility to recognize that you need to know how to ask after all those details.

* "Concept art can cause more problems than it solves."

1. Asking what you need that concepted asset for in the game, i.e. nailing down the asset's purpose. Maybe color and size for level artist, but also where they can place it, which the 3D modeler could care less about, but does want the concept to convey the materials and geometry needed by the asset. LDs will care about it for its functionality in their levels, parkour designers for its functionality relative to the player abilities. Getting all this info precedes the creation of the concept art.

2. Processes the old info from the concept's brief (i.e. feature request description) vs. the feedback from step one -- esp. looking for contradictions. e.g. LD needs it 170 cm tall, parkour designer needs 130. Can help both by showing the dumpster lid's slanted in the concept art -- but would never have known to do this without asking the asset's users. Making the art first wastes time, because it'll just kick off questions leading to an inevitable [total] rework of that art--so it's best to get the questions in prior to making concept art. Do the concepting before the art!

1. Answer more questions than you're creating.

2. Remember what you're doing and why.

3. Keep in mind who your concept art will affect!

All of these become tougher with larger teams!

Should it all be in the brief?

This blame game doesn't get the actual game any closer to completion -- which is your job foremost. This is the real humility for a concept artist: knowing you can stand in the way of all the passions, aspirations, hopes and funding behind a game's development with just one dumpster.

Premise Seeds

For Sci-Fi: Kate Messner

The 3 Story Seeds + How to Grow Them -- //My what-if ("plotter") approach is just one of three options!

1. Character creators: A character pops into their head and they write the book by following the character around. A famous example of this is J.K. Rowling, and her story about being on the train one day and basically seeing Harry Potter walk down the aisle.

//Develop by finding the world/scenario they have the most impact on given their fears/wants. Who would they surround themselves with? What community would that take place within? Then consider the conflict that reflects their fears/wants.

2. World builders: These are authors who find a time or place or some element of world building – like a system of magic – and go, “Ooh! I want to play with that!” Brandon Sanderson has talked several times about how he always figures out his magic systems first.

[!] //KEY TO NOTE: doesn't start on a grand scale, it begins from a small piece just as much as character-building does!

//Decide what part of the world you want to explore, and figure out all the rules that apply to that part of the world. Who lives there? What are their lives like? And what will throw that world into complete and utter turmoil? Once you’re in turmoil, how do you fix it?

3. Plotters: These are the people who go, “What if?” They start with the hook and build the book from there. Our own A Little Something Different is an example of this. What if we just had a traditional college love story, but what if it was told from the viewpoints of everyone around the main characters? Sandy Hall actually asked a teenage friend what book she wanted to read, and built her story from there.

//The thing about a “what if” is that one “what if” isn’t enough. The story comes from the conflict between two or more questions. And you’re not limited to two. Every “what if” leads to another “what if.” Before long, as you answer all these questions, you will start to get a better idea of your world and/or the main characters. Then you can refer to the suggestions above to flesh things out!
